STUDY AREAS: Baguio City Cebu City Davao City Iloilo City Mandaue City Quezon City Zamboanga City

Baguio City

The name Baguio conjures, for both the international and domestic traveler, a highland retreat in the Grand Cordillera in Northern Luzon, with pine trees, crisp cold breezes and low verdant knolls and hillocks. Through the numerous decades Baguio has morphed from what was once a grassy marshland into one of the cleanest and greenest, most highly urbanized cities in the country. It has made its mark as a premiere tourist destination in the Northern part of the Philippines with its cool climate, foggy hills, panoramic views and lovely flowers.

Being the ideal convergence zone of neighboring highland places, Baguio is the melting pot of different peoples and cultures and has boosted its ability to provide a center for education for its neighbors. Its rich culture and countless resources have lured numerous investments and business opportunities to the city.

Baguio City is the Philippines’ summer capital and is one of the 180 creative cities worldwide hailed by the United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization (UNESCO). The city which is the center of business, commerce and education in Northern Luzon. It is approximately 250 kilometers north of Manila, situated in the Province of Benguet.

Of the City’s total land area, only 467.5 ha (8.1%) still have old growths of pine while 1, 137.5 ha (19.8%) have production pine stands and 122.5 ha (2.1%) are brush lands. The remaining area of 4,021.2 ha making up 70% comprises the developed portion. The city comprises 129 barangays and has a population of 345,366 as of 2015.

On average, the temperature ranges from 15 to 23 °C. Baguio is 8 °C cooler on the average than any place in the lowlands of the country. The coldest months in Baguio City are usually November, December, January, and February, while the hottest months are March, April, and May. The city also receives the biggest amount of rainfall in the country, twice the volume of rainfall as compared to Manila.


Cebu City

Cebu City is a highly urbanized city located in the middle of the eastern coast of the island province of Cebu, in the Central Visayas Region of the Philippines. It is the seat of the provincial government although the city itself retains political independence from the provincial government. It comprises 80 barangays subdivided into two congressional districts. Barangays closer to the coast are generally smaller, more densely populated, and primarily residential or industrial while barangays towards the northwest are generally larger and less economically developed.

A feature of Cebu City, which is common to many cities and municipalities in the province of Cebu, is that its administrative boundary encloses an area with a large variation in elevation, going from sea level in the small, densely packed coastal barangays, to elevations of up to 900 meters above sea level towards the mountainous central spine of the island of Cebu. These mountain barangays generally have much smaller population densities, and although the primary source of livelihood is agriculture, the landscape at these higher elevations does not lend itself well to widespread cultivation.

The city is host to many business process outsourcing companies and is an important hub of tourism, trade, and industry in the region. The Port of Cebu is the second largest port in the Philippines, and due to the city’s geographic location in the center of the country, it has become a gateway for travel in the Visayas and Mindanao.


Davao City

Davao City is a highly-urbanized city located in the southeastern part of Mindanao. It is the capital city of the Davao Region and is the largest city in the Philippines in terms of land area with 244,000 hectares. The third-most populous city in the Philippines is Davao City after Quezon City and Manila. When the City of Davao began on March 1, 1937, its population was listed at about 68,000. By 2015, it had quadrupled to 1,632,991.

A large part of Davao City is mountainous, characterized by extensive mountain ranges with uneven distribution of highlands and lowlands. Home to the 11 tribes, the city is proud of its various cultures and traditions which makes it a diverse but united city for everyone to experience. Compared with other parts of the Philippines in which there is a distinct hot and wet season, Davao City experiences the privilege of a mild tropical climate where the days are always sunny and followed by nights of rain. It is outside the typhoon belt and lacks major seasonal variations. Its annual average temperatures range from 25.0 to 32.8 °C with the hottest occurring during the month of April, and the coldest occurring in January based on observations in 2018.

Davao City is composed of 3 congressional districts subdivided into 11 administrative districts with 182 barangays. It boasts itself as a globally livable city and a center of excellence in governance, investment, tourism, climate change adaptation, disaster resiliency, and sustainable growth driven by empowered citizenry.

The city’s missions include: ensure effective and efficient delivery of services through responsive leadership and competent human resources; sustain the gains by ensuring that the people of Davao are empowered, protected and secure, and gender and culturally sensitive to attain the best quality of life; continue to engage participative citizenry in resource-based, market-driven economic activities within the context of balanced ecology, and equity-led development; develop a progressive and future-proof network of infrastructure, utilities, and support services; and maintain a disaster-resilient, climate change-adaptive, and ecologically balanced environment through ridge-to-reef approach, proper solid waste management, preservation of natural environment, promotion of renewable energy, and adoption of low carbon and eco-friendly technologies.

The “Davao Life is Here” logo represents the five significant icons of Davao City: 1) the impressive Philippine eagle; 2) the breathtaking Mt. Apo; 3) the valued 11 ethnolinguistic tribes; 4) the enticing King of fruits, durian; and 5) the delightful Queen of Philippine flowers, waling-waling. All are signifying the bounty and richness of the city’s flora and fauna. The logo also means what the Davaoeños aspire and live for – unity amidst diversity.


Iloilo City

Dubbed as the “Heart of the Philippines”, Iloilo City is a 1st class highly urbanized city located at the southeastern part of Panay Island. It is considered as the provincial capital of Iloilo Province and the regional center and largest city in the Western Visayas Region. It is surrounded by the municipalities of Pavia in the north, Leganes in the northeast, and Oton in the west. In addition, the city is also bounded along the southeast by the Iloilo-Guimaras Strait, and within its territory lies three major water bodies, namely, the Iloilo River, Batiano River and Jaro River. The city covers a land area of approximately 78.34 square kilometers with a total population of 461,841 as of 2016. It is comprised of 180 barangays that are clustered into seven geographical districts, namely, Arevalo, City Proper, Jaro, La Paz, Lapuz, Mandurriao, and Molo.

The city has a monsoonal climate consisting of dry season from January to April and wet season from May to December. According to the 2010 climatological data from the Philippine Atmospheric, Geophysical and Astronomical Services Administration (PAG-ASA), the average temperature of the city throughout the year is 27.8 °C. Hottest temperatures in Iloilo City are usually experienced from March to May while its coldest months are from December to February.

Based on the 2011 – 2020 Comprehensive Land Use Plan of Iloilo City, majority of the city’s land area is occupied by residential zones (57.35%), followed by commercial zones (8.21%), and park and open spaces (5.50%). Moreover, approximately 4.01% of the land area are classified as fishponds while 1.36% are covered with mangroves. Among the most popular landmarks in the city is the Calle Real (officially known as J.M. Basa St.) located in the district of City Proper, which is dubbed as the “Escolta of Iloilo” and is one of the most preserved heritage business districts in the country. In addition, the city is also known for its green parks such as the Iloilo River Esplanade, which is the longest linear park and riverside promenade in the Philippines.


Mandaue City

Mandaue City is situated in the eastern coastal plains of Cebu province located at 10 9’ 40.34” N, 123 5’ 28.16” E with an average elevation of 16.8 meters above sea level. It has a land area of 3,487 hectares and is comprised of twenty-seven barangays. It is categorized as a Highly Urbanized City (HUC) and recognised as the most industrialized city in Metro Cebu (Castillo et. al, 2018). It is known as the focal point of manufacturing in the Philippines and is considered as the industrial hub in Cebu province hosting about 20,000 industrial and commercial establishments. Furthermore, the Mandaue local government unit (LGU) plans that by 2020 “Mandaue will be the Home of High Value Manufacturing Serving the Global Market”. Despite being a highly industrialized city, the Mandaue LGU envisions Mandaue City as a green city in the future.


Quezon City

Quezon City is a highly-urbanized city which was once the nation’s capital for almost 28 years. It is in the northeastern part of Metro Manila, bounded by the Novaliches Watershed in the northeast and by Marikina River to the east. It is composed of 6 districts with a total of 142 barangays. It is the largest among the sixteen cities and one municipality in the whole region of Metro Manila, with a land area of 161.12 sq. km, covering almost 25% of the region’s total area. The city’s topography is largely rolling with alternating ridges and lowlands. As of 2015, Quezon City is considered as the most populous city in the country having over 2.9 million population.

Based on the 2011 land use map, about 41.57% was residential developments which include socialized housing sites and informal settlements, 8.13% for commercial land use, 4.03% for special urban development, 5.53% for industrial, 6.90% for institutional, and 16.25% for parks and open space with 14% coming from La Mesa Dam Reservoir. Quezon City is also home to various government agencies including the House of Representatives, Office of the Ombudsman, Sandiganbayan, Commission on Audit, Commission on Human Rights, and 7 cabinet departments. It is also where the La Mesa Watershed is located which is the last forest of its size in the metropolis.

The climate in Science Garden station located in Quezon City is characterized as a type I climate based on the Modified Coronas Classification. It has two pronounced seasons, dry from November to April and wet during the rest of the year. Rainfall is at maximum during July and August which is due to the southwest monsoon (Habagat) and is minimum in February. The mean annual temperature is 27.7°C with January as the coolest month while May as the hottest.

Based on the 1981-2010 climatological normals of Science Garden Synoptic Station, its annual mean temperature is 27.7°C. January is the coolest month with an annual mean temperature of 25.7°C while the warmest month is May with an annual mean temperature of 29.7°C. Minimum temperature is observed in the month of January at 20.8°C on the average, while the maximum temperature is observed in the month of April at 35.0°C on the average. As of 2018, the highest temperature recorded in Science Garden was 38.5°C on May 14, 1987, while the lowest temperature recorded was 14.9°C on March 01, 1963.
